I get two kinds of messages.

The first: "Can you build this?" — and the answer is yes, but they could do 80% of it with a $30/month tool and a weekend.

The second: "I've been fighting this spreadsheet / no-code stack / half-built WordPress setup for nine months" — and the answer is also yes, but they should have called six months earlier.

This piece is the decision filter I wish more people used **before** they either overspend or under-build.

## Three lanes (not two)

Most people frame it as **DIY vs hire a developer**. Real life has three lanes:

1. **Do nothing special** — use a generic tool as designed
2. **Configure / DIY** — templates, Zapier-style glue, Sheets, site builders
3. **Custom build** — software shaped to *your* workflow

The money leaks when you pick the wrong lane for the problem's real shape.

## Stay in DIY when…

DIY (or cheap SaaS) is the right call when:

- **The workflow is standard.** Booking, invoicing, basic ecommerce, email capture — solved problems with good tools.
- **Failure is cheap.** If the process breaks, you can fall back to a phone call and a notebook for a week without losing the business.
- **You're still discovering the rules.** If you don't know what "done" looks like yet, a custom app will encode your confusion in code. Spreadsheets and simple tools are better for *figuring it out* first — same idea as [the spreadsheet-as-tool vs system line](/articles/the-spreadsheet-is-not-a-business-system).
- **The cost of a wrong custom build is higher than a year of software rent.** Custom is not automatically cheaper long-term if you overbuilt.

DIY is how most healthy small businesses start. That's fine.

## Hire (or commission) when…

You need a developer when one or more of these is true:

### 1. The tool is now load-bearing
If the spreadsheet / cobbled stack **going down stops the business**, you're past DIY theater. You need validation, multi-user safety, history, and something someone else can run without a tribal elder.

### 2. You're paying a weekly tax in hours
Add up the hours spent re-entering data, reconciling channels, copying between systems, fixing "someone sorted the wrong column." If that tax is real and recurring, automation or custom tooling often pays for itself — the same math as [inventory sync across channels](/articles/automate-inventory-sync-across-3-platforms).

### 3. Off-the-shelf forces a bad process
SaaS is opinionated. When the tool's opinions fight how you actually operate — and the workaround is three people and a prayer — configuration has hit a ceiling.

### 4. Compliance, money, or inventory accuracy is on the line
"Good enough" DIY that double-sells stock or leaks customer data is not frugal. It's deferred disaster.

### 5. You've already failed at DIY twice
Two abandoned builds is a signal. The problem isn't that you didn't try hard enough. The problem is the problem is hard.

## The expensive middle (avoid this)

The worst place is **half-custom, half-abandoned**:

- A developer who "started something" and disappeared
- A no-code app only one employee understands
- A WordPress plugin stack that no one will update because they're scared
- A site that is almost done, forever

That middle zone costs more than either clean DIY or a scoped professional build. It has all the fragility of custom and none of the ownership.

## How to hire without getting burned

If you do hire, make the engagement small and sharp:

1. **One workflow.** Not "digitize the company." One painful process.
2. **A written definition of done.** What can a user do on day one that they couldn't do before?
3. **Your current process as the spec.** The spreadsheet, the paper forms, the screenshots — that's the requirements doc you already wrote by living it.
4. **Access and ownership.** You get the code/repo/hosting, not a mystery black box.
5. **Maintenance clarity.** Who updates it? What does a change cost?

A good first project is boring on purpose: the appointment flow, the inventory sync, the quote request that actually lands in the right inbox. Boring is billable. Flashy is optional.

## A simple scorecard

Score each statement 0–2 (no / kind of / yes):

- This process runs daily or weekly and involves money, stock, or customers
- More than one person has to touch it
- Mistakes are expensive or embarrassing
- We've outgrown the current tool twice
- We can describe the happy path and the edge cases out loud

**0–3:** stay DIY / SaaS.  
**4–6:** configure harder or hire for a small automation.  
**7–10:** stop duct-taping. Commission a real fix.

## What I'm not saying

I'm not saying developers are always the answer. Plenty of "custom app" pitches are solutions looking for a budget. I'm also not saying you should be ashamed of DIY — I recommend it constantly.

I'm saying **match the tool to the job's seriousness**, and notice when the job changed while you were busy.
