"Free website" is the most successful marketing phrase in small-business tech, and it works because the first hour *does* feel free. Drag a block. Drop a photo. Publish something that looks better than the HTML you would've written in 2009.

Then the meter starts.

This is not a rant against website builders. For many businesses they're the right first move. It's a rant against **calling them free** when the real bill is money, time, and optionality.

## Cost #1 — the plan you actually need

The free tier exists to make you care. Want a custom domain without shame in the URL? Paid. Want to hide their ads / badge? Paid. Want commerce, bookings, or remove storage limits? Paid.

By the time a real business is "done," you're rarely on free. You're on a mid-tier annual plan — and that's fine **if you budgeted for it**. What's not fine is discovering it after you've already built the whole site around the platform.

## Cost #2 — the domain and email (which were never free)

A real business needs:

- A domain you control
- Email on that domain (`you@yourbusiness.com`, not `yourbusiness@gmail.com`)

Those are separate purchases/subscriptions whether you use a builder or not. Builders often bundle them in ways that make **leaving** harder later. Read the fine print on who owns the domain registration and how transfer works *before* you attach your brand to the account.

## Cost #3 — the design tax

Builders make average look easy and excellent look expensive.

You pay either in:

- **Hours** of you wrestling templates, fonts, and mobile breakpoints, or
- **Cash** for a designer who knows that platform's quirks, or
- **Brand damage** from a site that looks like every other template on the block

None of those show up on the pricing page as "design tax," but they dominate the first 90 days.

## Cost #4 — the performance and SEO ceiling

You can do local SEO and solid content on a builder. Plenty of businesses rank just fine.

You can also hit a wall: bloated page weight, limited control over technical SEO, plugins-for-everything thinking that recreates the [slow-site problems](/articles/why-your-small-business-website-feels-slow) without you realizing you installed them. Core Web Vitals still apply ([plain-English version here](/articles/core-web-vitals-explained-for-non-coders)). The platform's marketing will not prioritize your LCP over their app marketplace.

## Cost #5 — the exit tax (the one nobody mentions)

This is the real cost of "free."

When you outgrow the builder — custom workflow, headless setup, serious app, cleaner performance, multi-system integration — you discover:

- Content export is partial or ugly
- Design doesn't transfer
- URL structure may break
- Forms, members, and store data need migration projects
- Your "website" was never a portable asset; it was a **lease**

Leaving can cost more than building the next site — because you're paying to escape, not just to build.

That's the same rent-vs-own tension as [Square vs Shopify vs building it yourself](/articles/square-vs-shopify-vs-building-it-yourself-cost-2026): the platform is great until the platform is the constraint.

## A honest cost model (illustrative)

For a typical local service business on a mainstream builder for **year one**:

| Line item | Rough range |
|-----------|-------------|
| Required paid plan | low–mid hundreds / year |
| Domain | tens / year |
| Professional email | tens–low hundreds / year |
| Premium templates / apps / bookings add-ons | zero–several hundred |
| Your time (setup + rework) | the largest line if you count it |
| Designer / developer help | zero–a few thousand if you hire |

None of that makes builders "bad." It makes **"free" a fiction.**

## When a builder is still the right call

Use a builder when:

- You need **something credible this month**
- Your needs are standard (brochure + contact + light booking)
- You accept the lease model for 1–2 years while you learn what the business actually needs
- You will not pretend the free tier is a long-term home

That's rational. Temporary rent can be smart.

## When to stop

Start planning an exit when:

- You're fighting the tool weekly
- You need integrations the marketplace can't do cleanly
- Speed/SEO is costing leads
- The business is load-bearing on the site and you want real ownership

## What to do instead of feeling tricked

1. Budget the **real** annual cost on day one (plan + domain + email + apps).
2. Keep a copy of your content somewhere portable (docs, markdown, CMS export).
3. Own the domain at a registrar you control.
4. Don't build unique business logic inside a page builder if you can help it.
5. Revisit the lease once a year: still worth it, or time to own?
