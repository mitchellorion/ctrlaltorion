Somewhere along the way, a chat bubble became a default part of "looking like a real business" — right next to the cookie banner, the review popup, and the email-capture modal that hits you on second one.

Defaults are dangerous. Chat is a tool with a cost: **performance, attention, and a staffing promise.**

## What chat is actually for

Live chat wins when a buyer has a **blocking question** and a fast answer raises conversion:

- "Do you service my zip code?"  
- "Is this in stock / can you do Saturday?"  
- "What's the difference between plan A and B?"

It's weaker when the visitor needed a clear page, a price range, or a phone number — and you substituted a bubble for clarity.

If your [service page](/articles/how-to-write-a-service-page-that-converts) and [contact path](/articles/why-your-contact-form-is-silently-losing-leads) are weak, chat becomes a band-aid on a content problem.

## Cost #1 — speed and Core Web Vitals

Third-party chat scripts are routinely heavy. They load fonts, sockets, presence indicators, and marketing trackers. On a phone, that tax shows up as lag — the same class of problem as death-by-a-thousand-scripts in [why your site feels slow](/articles/why-your-small-business-website-feels-slow).

If chat is loaded on every page for every visitor, you're paying performance rent even when nobody chats.

**Mitigations if you keep it:**

- Load on click ("Chat with us") instead of always-on iframe  
- Restrict to high-intent pages (pricing, contact, product)  
- Defer until after the main content paints  

A bubble that costs you LCP/INP so you can answer "what are your hours?" is a bad trade when hours could be text on the page.

## Cost #2 — the staffing promise

A green "online" dot is a contract.

If the contract is "we'll reply in three hours" while the UI says "typically replies instantly," you train distrust. Visitors who wait, get silence, and bounce don't leave a tidy analytics event called *betrayed by chat*. They just leave.

Honest modes:

- **Staffed live hours** with real humans  
- **Async chat** labeled as such ("we reply within a business day")  
- **No chat** — phone + form only  

Lying with a green dot is worse than a static page.

## Cost #3 — mobile annoyance

On a small screen, persistent bubbles cover CTAs, collide with thumb zones, and stack with other widgets. If your primary button is "Book" and a chat puck sits on top of it, congratulations: you invented anti-conversion design.

Check your site on a real phone. If you need to dismiss UI to act, fix the UI.

## When chat helps (keep / add)

Keep chat when most of these are true:

- Someone can answer during advertised hours  
- Questions are frequent and similar (good candidates for canned replies + a better FAQ)  
- Average order or job value justifies interrupt-driven sales  
- You measured that chat transcripts turn into booked work  
- You've already made hours, pricing signal, and service area obvious without chat  

## When chat hurts (remove / replace)

Remove or replace when:

- Nobody's on the other end most of the day  
- The script tanks mobile performance scores you care about  
- Chat becomes a dumping ground for support you should systematize  
- You're hiding basic info behind a conversation on purpose  
- Your best customers prefer call/text and you already know that  

A strong replacement stack for many local businesses:

1. Click-to-call  
2. Short form  
3. SMS  
4. Clear FAQ on the page  

Boring. Effective. Staffable.

## The hybrid that usually wins

- **No always-on third-party widget on every page**  
- **Click-to-chat or "message us" that opens SMS or a lightweight form**  
- **Business hours stated in text**  
- **Canned answers for the top five questions published as page content**

You're still reachable. You're just not paying a JavaScript tax to look like a call center.

## A one-week experiment

If you already have chat:

1. Export two weeks of transcripts.  
2. Tag each as sales / support / spam / silence.  
3. Count hours to first human reply.  
4. Note which questions should have been page content.  
5. Run a PageSpeed test with chat blocked vs enabled.  

If sales-qualified chats are rare, replies are slow, and performance jumps when blocked — you have your answer.

## What I'm not saying

I'm not saying chat is scams-and-fools. Ecommerce at scale, complex B2B, and high-touch sales teams use it well.

I'm saying **most small sites installed the bubble because a template did**, then never staffed the promise or measured the return.
