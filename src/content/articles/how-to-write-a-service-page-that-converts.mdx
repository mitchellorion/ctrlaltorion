Open ten local service pages at random and you'll see the same crime scene:

A hero image of a handshake. A headline that says "Quality You Can Trust." Three icons (Fast, Affordable, Professional). A paragraph that starts with "We are a family-owned…" And a form at the bottom that asks for your life story before you've been given a reason to type.

Nobody converts on that. People *endure* it.

A service page that works is built like a good consult call: **problem → fit → proof → process → price signal → objection handling → next step.**

## Before you write: one page, one job

One service page should sell **one primary service** (or one tight package) to **one primary buyer**.

"Plumbing Services" can work for a small shop. "Plumbing + Remodeling + HVAC + Electrical + Handyman" on one page is five sales conversations fighting in a trench coat. Split them. This pairs with the [five-page site structure](/articles/five-page-website-every-local-business-needs): Home greets, Services sells.

## The section order that converts

### 1. Headline: who + what + where (or outcome)

Not "Welcome." Not your company name again.

Patterns that work:

- **[Service] for [who] in [place]**
- **[Outcome] without [pain]**
- **[Service] — [time/availability promise you can keep]**

If the visitor only reads this line, they should know whether they're in the right place.

### 2. Subhead: the specific promise

One or two sentences. What happens if they hire you? Same-day diagnostic? Fixed-scope packages? Licensed + insured with photos of the work?

### 3. Primary CTA above the fold

Call, book, or request estimate — **one** primary action. Secondary can exist (chat, text). Primary must be obvious on a phone with a thumb.

### 4. "Is this for me?" section

Bullet the situations you solve. People scan for themselves. "Burst pipe / water heater replacement / repipe estimate" beats a paragraph about craftsmanship.

### 5. How it works (3–5 steps)

Anxiety drops when the process is visible: request → confirm → on-site → quote → done. Unclear process is why people ghost forms.

### 6. Proof (not vibes)

Pick two or three:

- Real photos of *this* service (not generic stock)
- Review quotes that mention the service
- Licenses, guarantees, years, "as seen in" only if real
- A short case note with numbers ("panel upgrade, SE Portland, one day")

Proof belongs *before* the long form, not as a footer afterthought.

### 7. Price signal (even if you hate it)

You don't need a public menu for every custom job. You *do* need to reduce the "is this $200 or $8,000?" panic.

Options:

- Starting-at prices
- Package tiers
- "Typical range for X"
- What affects cost (so the range makes sense)

Silence on price feels like a trap. A range feels like respect.

### 8. Objections, answered in plain language

Write the questions people text their spouse:

- How soon can you come?
- Do you guarantee the work?
- Are you insured?
- What area do you serve?
- What happens if something goes wrong?

FAQ works here if it's honest. Fake FAQ ("What makes you the best?") is noise.

### 9. Final CTA + low-friction contact

Repeat the primary action. Keep form fields minimal: name, contact, short description, zip/service area. Every extra field is a filter against busy people. Then make sure the form actually works — see [why contact forms silently lose leads](/articles/why-your-contact-form-is-silently-losing-leads).

## SEO without writing like a robot

Yes, put the service and city in the title, H1, and naturally in the body. No, don't write "plumber Portland plumber Portland plumber."

Useful local content wins: service area, what you actually do, photos, clear NAP consistency with your [Google Business Profile](/articles/why-your-google-business-profile-isnt-showing-up). The [local SEO checklist](/articles/local-seo-checklist-nobody-gives-you-free) is the system; this page is the on-site workhorse.

## Words to delete on sight

- "Quality," "excellence," "synergy," "one-stop shop" (unless you prove it)
- "Our team of dedicated professionals…"
- "In today's fast-paced world…"
- Any sentence you wouldn't say out loud to a customer in your shop

If it sounds like a template, it converts like a template.

## A 45-minute rewrite process

1. Open your current service page.
2. Write the buyer's top five questions on paper.
3. Check whether each is answered **above** the form.
4. Rewrite the headline last (after you know the real offer).
5. Cut half the adjectives. Add one proof block and one price signal.
6. Test on your phone. Can you take the next step in two taps?

## What I'm not saying

I'm not saying clever copy beats a bad offer. If you're slower, ruder, or more expensive with no upside, a better page just fails faster — which is still useful information.

I'm saying most service pages fail *before* the offer is judged, because the page never becomes a conversation.
