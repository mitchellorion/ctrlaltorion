Nobody ever decided to spend $400 a month on software. That's the thing about subscription sprawl — it never arrives as a decision. It arrives as a dozen small, reasonable "$15/month, that's nothing" moments spread across two years, and one day you look at the card statement and realize you're funding a small software company's payroll to do jobs that overlap.

I'm not anti-software. Good tools earn their keep. But most small businesses are paying for the *same capability three times* because each tool was signed up for in isolation, and nobody ever laid them all out side by side. So let's do that. This is a 30-minute audit that routinely finds a few hundred dollars a month nobody was using on purpose.

## Step 1 — Get them all in one list

You can't see overlap until everything's visible. Pull up your card and bank statements for the last two months and write down **every recurring software charge**: name, what it does, monthly cost. Don't skip the annual ones — divide by twelve and add them.

Two things will happen during this step, and both are valuable:

1. You'll find **zombie subscriptions** — tools you stopped using months ago that are still billing. Cancel those on the spot; that's free money you're already not using.
2. You'll find charges you **don't recognize.** Track them down. A mystery $29/month is either a forgotten tool or a problem, and both need answering.

## Step 2 — Group by what they actually *do*

Now sort the list not by name but by **job.** This is where the overlap jumps out. The usual clusters:

- **File storage** — somehow you're paying for Dropbox *and* Google Drive *and* OneDrive, when one of them (probably the one bundled with email you already pay for) would cover everything.
- **Email marketing / newsletters** — a standalone email tool, *plus* the email feature built into your CRM, *plus* the one in your e-commerce platform. Three sends, one job.
- **Scheduling / booking** — Calendly and Acuity and the booking widget your site platform already includes.
- **Design / graphics** — two or three "make a graphic" subscriptions when one does it all.
- **Invoicing / payments** — a standalone invoicing app, plus invoicing inside your accounting software, plus what your POS already does.
- **Project/task management** — the classic graveyard, where teams pay for three because they keep "trying" a new one and never cancel the last.

When two tools land in the same job bucket, you've found a candidate to cut. Usually one is clearly better, or one is *already included* in something else you pay for.

## Step 3 — Check what your big tools already include

This is the step that finds the most money. The platforms you already pay for are bundles, and you're probably buying their features *a second time* elsewhere:

- **Google Workspace / Microsoft 365** already give you storage, video calls, and document collaboration — so why the separate Dropbox, Zoom, and Notion?
- **Your e-commerce or POS platform** likely includes basic email, discounts, and reporting you might be paying a separate tool to do.
- **Your accounting software** very likely does invoicing and maybe expense tracking you're paying a standalone app for.

Half of subscription sprawl isn't extra tools — it's *paying twice* for features you already own inside a platform you forgot was that capable.

## Step 4 — Cut, but keep what earns its line

To be clear, the goal is not "cancel everything and suffer." Some subscriptions are worth every cent — the one tool that does a critical job better than anything else, the thing your whole workflow runs on. Keep those proudly.

The test for each subscription is simple: **does this earn its line on the statement, or is it here because canceling felt like effort?** Keep the earners. Cut the duplicates, the zombies, and the "it's only $15" tools whose job something else already does. Move what you can into the platforms you already pay for.

## When the answer isn't "cancel" — it's "replace five with one"

Here's the pattern I see at the end of these audits. Sometimes you're not paying for redundant tools — you're paying for **five tools that each do 70% of what you need**, glued together with manual copy-paste, because no single off-the-shelf product fits your actual workflow. You're renting a stack of almost-fits and paying in both money *and* the time it takes to make them talk to each other.

That's the moment a single, small, **purpose-built tool** can replace the whole tangle — one thing that does exactly your workflow, end to end, for less than the combined rent of the five it retires. It's the same logic as [graduating a load-bearing spreadsheet](/articles/the-spreadsheet-is-not-a-business-system): when the patchwork costs more than the custom, the custom wins.
