Every local business that asks for a "full website" walks in with the same mental picture: Home, About, Services, Gallery, Blog, FAQ, Team, Careers, Testimonials, Locations, and a Contact page that somehow still doesn't convert.

That's not a website. That's a filing cabinet.

What most local businesses actually need is **five pages that each do one job**. Anything past that is optional until the five are working.

## Why five (not "as many as you can think of")

A website is not a museum of everything you've ever done. It's a **sales conversation that runs when you're not in the room**.

The visitor arrives with three questions:

1. Do you do the thing I need?
2. Are you credible / nearby / real?
3. How do I take the next step?

If those answers take seven clicks and a blog archive, you lose people. Five focused pages can answer all three without the bloat — and they're cheaper to keep fast, which matters for [how your site actually feels to customers](/articles/why-your-small-business-website-feels-slow).

## The five pages

### 1. Home — the handshake

**Job:** Make it obvious who you help, what you do, and what to do next in under five seconds.

Not a carousel of stock photos. Not a vague mission statement. A clear headline ("Emergency plumbing in Portland — same-day when it matters"), one primary CTA (call / book / request estimate), and enough proof to keep scrolling (reviews, years in business, service area).

If someone lands on Home and still has to guess whether you serve their city or their problem, the page failed.

### 2. Services (or one page per main service)

**Job:** Match the search intent and remove ambiguity.

This is usually the page that should rank and convert. Name the service the way customers say it. Explain what it includes, what it costs in ranges if you can, who it's for, and what happens after they click. If you do three very different things, three service pages beat one vague "What We Offer" blob.

This is also where thin, empty service pages lose — if you need the full playbook, start with [how to write a service page that converts](/articles/how-to-write-a-service-page-that-converts).

### 3. About / Proof

**Job:** Make you real.

People don't need your childhood story. They need reasons to trust you: face, location, licenses, insurance, real photos of the work, a few sentences of personality that aren't corporate mush. Local buyers are hiring a human. Show one.

### 4. Work / Results / Gallery

**Job:** Show the outcome without making them take your word for it.

Before/after, project snapshots, short case notes ("kitchen remodel, Westside, 3 weeks"). Skip the 80-photo dump with no captions. Ten strong examples with context beat a slideshow that never loads on mobile.

### 5. Contact

**Job:** Make the next step stupidly easy — and make sure it actually works.

Phone click-to-call on mobile. Form with only the fields you need. Hours. Service area. Map if you're storefront. Confirmation that the message was received. This page fails more often than people admit — dead forms, no autoresponse, spam folder delivery — which is its own problem ([contact forms silently losing leads](/articles/why-your-contact-form-is-silently-losing-leads)).

## What you can skip (for now)

- **A blog** — unless you will actually publish useful local content on a schedule. An empty "News" page from 2019 is worse than no blog.
- **A Careers page** — until you're hiring.
- **A FAQ that duplicates Services** — put objections on the service page where decisions happen.
- **Ten location pages** that say the same thing with the city name swapped — Google is not stupid, and neither are people.

## The structure in one line

**Home greets → Services sells → About/Work proves → Contact closes.**

Everything else is an expansion pack for when the core is converting and you have a real reason to grow the site.

## How this ties to local SEO

These five pages are also the right skeleton for local search. Your service pages and Home should carry city + service language; your NAP should match your [Google Business Profile](/articles/why-your-google-business-profile-isnt-showing-up); your Contact page should not contradict your listings. The [local SEO checklist](/articles/local-seo-checklist-nobody-gives-you-free) is mostly about consistency and a few high-weight signals — not inventing twenty pages of fluff.

## What I'm not saying

I'm not saying every business is only five pages forever. Multi-location brands, ecommerce catalogs, and content businesses need more. I'm saying **most single-location service businesses are overbuilding** and under-converting because they treated the website like a company brochure instead of a tool.

## Do this next

1. List the pages you have.
2. Map each one to a job above — or admit it has no job.
3. Merge or kill the orphans.
4. Make sure each of the five has one clear CTA and loads fast on a phone.
