Ask five people what a small-business website costs and you'll get answers from "free, I'll do it on Wix" to "we paid an agency $40,000." The maddening part is that *they're all telling the truth.* The price of a website is not one number, because "a website" isn't one thing. A digital business card and a booking system that runs your calendar are both "websites" the way a bicycle and a delivery van are both "vehicles."

So let's do this honestly. Here are the real 2026 price tiers, what each one actually buys, and — the part nobody quotes you up front — what it costs to keep the thing running after launch.

## The four real tiers

### Tier 1 — DIY website builder: ~$200–600/year
Wix, Squarespace, Shopify, and friends. You pay a subscription (roughly **$16–50/month** depending on plan and whether you're selling) and you build it yourself from templates. {/* [VERIFY] current builder plan pricing at publish */}

The sticker price is low, but **the real cost is your time** — realistically 20–40 hours to get something decent, and it'll look like a template because it is one. Perfectly fine for a brochure site, a portfolio, a simple shop. This is the right tier for a lot of businesses, and there's no shame in it.

### Tier 2 — Template + a freelancer: ~$500–3,000 one-time
You hire someone to set up and customize a template for you. You're buying their **speed and taste**, not custom engineering — they start from an existing theme and make it yours. Good value when you want it to look professional and don't want to spend your own 40 hours. Quality varies wildly with the freelancer, so judge by their portfolio, not their rate.

### Tier 3 — Professional custom build: ~$5,000–25,000+
Now you're paying for **custom design and real functionality** — a site built for your business specifically, with the things a template can't do well: a booking system, inventory-aware product pages, integrations with your POS or CRM, custom workflows, performance built in from the start. The number climbs with complexity. A custom brochure site sits near the bottom; anything that *does* something — takes bookings, syncs stock, handles members — sits higher.

This is the tier where you stop buying a website and start buying a **tool that runs part of your business.**

### Tier 4 — Agency: ~$25,000–100,000+
A full agency with designers, developers, strategists, project managers. Appropriate for funded startups and established companies with real budgets and complex needs. For most small businesses, this is more process and overhead than the problem requires — you're paying for the org chart as much as the site.

## What actually drives the number

Within any tier, the price moves based on:

- **How many pages** and how much custom design each needs.
- **E-commerce** — selling online adds payment, product, tax, and shipping complexity.
- **Functionality** — a booking system, member logins, a searchable catalog, or any integration with tools you already use. This is the biggest multiplier. "A website" is cheap; "a website that *does* X" is where cost lives.
- **Content** — who writes the copy and shoots the photos? If it's them, it's in the bill.
- **Integrations** — every system the site has to talk to (POS, inventory, email, CRM) is engineering, not decoration.

If you've read [our breakdown of Square vs. Shopify vs. building it yourself](/articles/square-vs-shopify-vs-building-it-yourself-cost-2026), this is the same logic one level up: the more your site has to *do*, the more it costs, and the question is always whether to rent that capability or build it.

## The costs nobody quotes you

Here's where people get blindsided. The build is a one-time number; **running** the site is forever:

- **Domain name** — ~$15/year. Small but perpetual.
- **Hosting** — anywhere from free to ~$50/month depending on the site. Builders bundle it into the subscription; custom sites need it separately.
- **Maintenance** — updates, security patches, plugin upkeep, the occasional fix. Budget for it whether you do it yourself (time) or pay someone (money). A site is a garden, not a statue.
- **SSL certificate** — effectively free now on most setups, but confirm it's included; a site without HTTPS gets punished by browsers and Google alike.

The honest rule: **whatever the build costs, plan for ongoing costs on top.** A "cheap" site with neglected maintenance becomes an expensive security incident.

## So what *should* you pay?

The right answer is the cheapest tier that does the job your site actually has to do:

- **Just need to exist and look credible?** Tier 1 or 2. Don't overspend on a brochure.
- **Need the site to *do* something** — bookings, real inventory, integrations, a workflow that's currently manual? Tier 3. A template fighting your workflow costs more in the long run than a build that fits it.
- **Funded, complex, and scaling?** Tier 4.

Most small businesses overspend in one of two ways: paying agency money for a brochure, or duct-taping a $30/month builder into doing a $10,000 job it will never do well. Match the tier to the job and you'll spend the right amount.
